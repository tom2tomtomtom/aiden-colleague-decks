Mariana
intent 3/10
skeptical

leaned back slightly, one eyebrow raised, occasionally glancing away as if already half-checked out

Honestly, this feels like every other supermarket ad trying to position themselves as the calm antidote to shopping chaos. The 'dissolving' visual transition is pretty tired at this point—seen it in tea ads, insurance ads, meditation apps. And 'Good Different' as an endline? That's been ALDI's thing for ages, so there's nothing new here. The insight about discount fatigue might be real, but the execution sounds incredibly safe and focus-grouped.

This concept fundamentally misunderstands what makes ALDI actually work for people like me. The whole premise—that other discount stores are chaotic and ALDI is serene—feels like it came from a brand workshop rather than real shopping behavior. ALDI is chaotic too, just differently chaotic. The appeal isn't some zen shopping experience, it's that their limited range actually works and you're not being played. The 'stressed shopper overwhelmed by offers' is such a lazy insight. We're not idiots who can't handle promotions—we're suspicious of them because they're usually bullshit. If ALDI wanted to own something real, they'd lean into the fact that they just price things fairly from the start instead of this manufactured calm-versus-chaos narrative. The whole thing feels like it's trying to make ALDI respectable and premium-adjacent, which completely misses why it's good. It's good because it's unpretentious and straightforward, not because it's some oasis of mindful shopping. This reeks of trying to appeal to everyone and ending up meaning nothing.

Works: The insight about promotional fatigue has some truth to it; ALDI's actual pricing model does avoid the fake discount trap

Concerns: Misrepresents what makes ALDI genuinely appealing—it's not about serenity; Visual transition feels derivative and overused in advertising; Trying to make ALDI something aspirational when its strength is being straightforward

Would convince: Show me actual ALDI shoppers talking about why they switched—not actors in a metaphorical calm space. Give me specifics: actual price comparisons, the limited range as a feature not a bug, the weird middle aisle finds. Lean into ALDI's irreverence instead of trying to make it respectable. If you're going to own 'Good Different,' show me what's actually different beyond just 'we don't do fake promotions.' Make it about intelligence and being in on something, not escaping chaos.

Different how? Good by what measure?

This is lazy advertising dressed up as insight. Yes, supermarket promotions can be confusing - I've got eyes, I've seen the multi-buys that aren't actually savings. But the solution isn't to stop thinking and just trust ALDI. That's precisely the kind of 'don't worry your pretty head' marketing that gets my back up. What they're actually saying is 'we're cheaper' but they won't come out and say it directly, so instead we get this vague 'confidence' nonsense. I shop at Waitrose mostly, sometimes Sainsbury's. Not because I'm a snob, but because I know what I'm getting. ALDI might be fine, but this ad gives me zero reason to change. No specifics on quality, range, or actual price comparison. Just emotional manipulation about feeling overwhelmed. I'm not overwhelmed. I'm busy, and this wastes my time by not telling me anything concrete.

Concerns: No concrete information about what makes ALDI actually better - just vague emotional claims; Patronising assumption that shoppers are overwhelmed rather than capable of making informed choices; 'Good Different' is meaningless jargon that tells me nothing

Would convince: Actual evidence. Independent quality testing results. Specific price comparisons on branded equivalent products. Customer retention data. Something measurable and verifiable, not just a feeling. And drop the tagline - tell me plainly what you offer and why it's worth switching.
