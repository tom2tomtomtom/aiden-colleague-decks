The Casting Instinct
2/10

This brief tells me nothing about who should make this. A 'warm and trustworthy' TikTok campaign could need a comedy writer who understands cringe, or a documentary director who finds real stories, or a creator who's already in the health anxiety conversation. The brief is so vague it could justify any team, which means it justifies none. I can't cast what I can't see.

Fix: Write one sentence describing the emotional register of the work—not 'warm,' but the actual feeling. Is it darkly funny? Radically honest? Absurdly optimistic? That sentence will tell me who to call.

The Line-Holder
3/10

The client wants to 'be part of the conversation' but hasn't said what conversation. Gen Z talks about mental health burnout, financial anxiety, medical gaslighting, the healthcare system as a scam. Which one is this brand brave enough to enter? Because if the answer is 'none, we just want to feel good,' then we're being hired to make expensive wallpaper.

Fix: Force the client to name the conversation they want to join and prove they have a right to be in it. If they can't, walk.

The Disproportionate Ambition
4/10

'Hero film plus cutdowns' is the format every brand requests because it sounds like a campaign. But TikTok doesn't work like that. The platform rewards serial narrative, comment-section dialogue, creator authenticity. This brief is asking for a TV ad chopped into vertical video. That's not disproportionate—it's just expensive mediocrity.

Fix: Reframe deliverables as a content system, not a film. What's the repeatable idea that can live for months, not the one-off hero moment that dies in a week?

The Structural Honesty
1/10

There's no budget, no timeline, no KPIs. Which means either the client doesn't trust us with the real constraints, or they haven't decided what success looks like. Both are fatal. We can't cast a team, negotiate rates, or define scope without knowing if this is a £20k experiment or a £200k brand play.

Fix: Get the budget and timeline in writing before another conversation happens. If they won't share it, they're not ready to work with us.

Strategic analysis

This isn't a brief. It's a wish list written by someone who's never opened TikTok.

'Make health insurance feel relevant' isn't an objective—it's the entire category's existential crisis dressed up as a campaign goal. 'Warm and trustworthy' is what every insurance brand says because they're all terrified of being what they actually are: necessary, expensive, and confusing. And 'feel viral and shareable'? You can't brief virality. You can only create conditions for it by having something genuine to say.

The real problem: there's no decision about what this brand believes that would make Gen Z care. No tension identified. No human truth about why 18-30 year olds avoid health insurance (spoiler: because they feel invincible and broke). No cultural territory staked out. Just a vague hope that TikTok's algorithm will do the strategy work for you.

The single biggest fix: Name the specific barrier this campaign will break. Is it 'health insurance feels like admitting you're old'? Is it 'I can't afford to think about my future when I can't afford rent'? Pick one enemy and destroy it.

Sharpened rewrite

Brief: Health Insurance for People Who Think They're Immortal

The Problem We're Solving

Gen Z knows they should care about health insurance. They just can't afford to—emotionally or financially. Caring feels like admitting you're old, boring, or broken. So they scroll past it, even while doomscrolling WebMD at 2am.

The Tension

Gen Z is the most health-anxious generation in history (chronically online, hyper-aware of symptoms, mental health fluent) AND the least likely to have health insurance (broke, invincible, living in the present because the future feels fake).

What We're Making

A TikTok content system that lives in that tension. Not a hero film. A serialized idea that can run for months, respond to culture, and invite participation. Format TBD based on the idea, but it must be platform-native: creator-led, comment-section-aware, algorithmically sustainable.

The Audience

22-28 year olds who:
- Google their symptoms but not their insurance options
- Talk about therapy but not primary care
- Know the system is broken but don't know what to do about it
- Want brands to be honest, not warm

The Brand (NEEDS FILLING)

[Brand name], a health insurance company that [what makes you different from every other health insurance company]. If there's no good answer to that, we need to create one through this campaign.

Tone

Not 'warm and trustworthy.' That's what scared brands say. Instead: radically honest, darkly funny, and genuinely useful. The tone of someone who gets that the system is absurd but is trying to help you navigate it anyway.

Success Looks Like

[X] sign-ups from 18-30 demographic in [timeline]
[X]% engagement rate (not just views—saves, shares, comment dialogue)
Evidence we've shifted perception: 'health insurance' becomes associated with [specific new feeling] not [old baggage]
Qualitative: We become the brand Gen Z quotes when talking about healthcare

What We Need to Proceed

Budget: [amount]
Timeline: [dates]
Brand positioning deck or honest admission there isn't one
Three verbatim quotes from your target audience about why they don't have health insurance
The one conversation you're brave enough to enter

The Enemy

Euphemism. Jargon. The pretense that health insurance is anything other than a necessary evil in a broken system. If we're not willing to acknowledge that, we have no right to ask Gen Z to trust us.
