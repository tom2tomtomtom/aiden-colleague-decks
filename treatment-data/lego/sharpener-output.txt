The Child's Logic
2/10

You're building for adults pretending to understand kids. 'Warm and trustworthy' is what a compliance team thinks Gen Z wants. Actual Gen Z doesn't trust warm—they trust real. They share things that make them look smart or feel seen, not things that make a brand 'feel good.' This brief is designed to please the client, not move the audience.

Fix: Stop trying to make insurance cool. Find the exact moment it becomes necessary—the first ER bill, the pharmacy counter panic, the 26th birthday when you age off your parents' plan—and make THAT moment the story.

The Permanence Anxiety
3/10

'Viral and shareable' is the language of disposability. You're chasing a moment instead of building a system. What happens after the hero film? What does cutdown #4 connect to? This brief has no architecture. It's asking for a one-off that'll get 48 hours of attention and then vanish, teaching the audience nothing about why this brand matters.

Fix: Design for sequence, not virality. The hero film should set up a question or tension that the cutdowns answer in different ways. Make each piece a brick that connects to the others.

The Audience Advocate
1/10

There's no evidence anyone has spoken to an actual 18-year-old. 'Make health insurance feel relevant' is a brief written from the brand's anxiety, not the audience's reality. What do they already think about health insurance? What do they call it? When do they think about it? The brief doesn't know and doesn't care.

Fix: Go find five 22-year-olds and ask them the last time they thought about health insurance. Record what they say verbatim. Rebuild the brief from their language, not yours.

Strategic analysis

This isn't a brief. It's a wish list written by someone who's never opened TikTok.

The core problem: You're asking Gen Z to engage with the least sexy category in existence (health insurance) using the language of brand committees ('warm and trustworthy,' 'part of the conversation'). Nobody on TikTok wants to be part of YOUR conversation. They want you to be part of theirs. The brief confuses 'viral' (an outcome you can't control) with 'shareable' (a quality you can design for), and offers zero insight into why an 18-year-old would give half a second to this.

What's missing: Any evidence you understand the audience. Any tension worth exploiting. Any reason this brand has permission to speak. 'Feel relevant' isn't a strategy—it's an admission you're currently irrelevant. The brief needs to name the specific moment in a Gen Z life where health insurance stops being a parent's problem and becomes theirs. That moment has texture, emotion, and a POV. Find it.

The Moment You Realize You're On Your Own

The Truth:
There's a specific moment when health insurance stops being your parents' problem and becomes yours. It's not gradual. It's sudden. You're at a pharmacy counter and your card gets declined. You're filling out paperwork at urgent care and you don't know your policy number. You turn 26 and age off your parents' plan and suddenly you're Googling 'what is a deductible' at 11pm. That moment—the system shock of realizing you're actually responsible for your own body now—is where this lives.

The Tension:
Gen Z knows they need health insurance. But engaging with it feels like admitting they're adults, and they're not ready for that. Insurance is the least sexy, most administrative part of growing up. It's paperwork and phone trees and terms they don't understand. So they avoid it until they can't.

What We're Actually Selling:
Not coverage. Not peace of mind. The ability to handle that moment without panic. [Brand] is the insurance that doesn't make you feel stupid for not knowing how insurance works.

The Work:
A TikTok series that captures the exact moments when health insurance becomes real. Not explainers. Not testimonials. Short, honest films about the pharmacy panic, the ER bill shock, the 26th birthday dread. Each one ends with a simple truth: 'You're going to need this. We'll make it simple.'

Hero film (60s): The montage of moments. Cutdowns (15-30s): Individual moments, each one shareable because it's painfully recognizable.

Tone:
Honest, not warm. Competent, not cute. The brand voice is the friend who's been through it and can explain it without making you feel dumb.

Success:
Not virality. Recognition. The metric is: does someone see this and think 'that was me'? Secondary: does it drive traffic to a landing page that actually helps them understand their options?

What We're Not Doing:
We're not trying to make insurance cool. We're not riding trends. We're not making the brand the hero. The person figuring this out is the hero. We're just the guide.
